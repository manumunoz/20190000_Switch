Motivation:
Group identity

  • • It is a key explanatory variable of social and economic behavior and outcomes [Akerlof & Kranton, 2000]
    1. - Social exclusion: limited access to opportunities[McPherson et al, 2001]
    2. - Economic inequality: limited access to resources [Chen & Li, 2009]
  • • Preferences for in-group over out-group[Tajfel & Turner, 1979]
Dual Role of Group Identity
They play the role of keycards that give individuals access to relations and resources with similar others (social integration), but also the role of straitjackets that keep individuals trapped within their group and hinder them from accessing opportunities from those who are different (social regulation).

Integration & Regulation (Durkheim, 1987)

  • Social Integration: Extent to which express their identification with social groups.
  • Social Regulation: Ability of a group to guide/constrain its members.

Chosen Group Identities

  • If fixed, individuals would be trapped in inefficient group dynamics [Hoff & Stiglitz, 2011]
  • Constructivist: Identities can change as the product of human decision making [Chandra, 2012]

I study the extent to which the choice over their group identity can help individuals break free from inefficient dynamics, looking at the effect of social integration and social regulation as key determinants of this process

What do we know?


Individuals can strategically choose their group identities (dependent variable)
  • Biracial children change ethnic groups identification when one of their parents belongs to a disadvantaged category [Davenport, 2016]
  • Individuals change their group identities to match an ideal political prototype [Egan, 2018]
  • African-American students joined study groups to improve academic performance were rejected by their peers for “acting white” [Austen-Smith & Fryer, 2005]

Shared (minimal) group identities help solve difficult problems (coordination games)
  • People with common (exogenous) group identities select more efficient equilibria [Chen & Chen, 2011]
  • If conflicting preferences, identities make efficient equilibria less likely (due to salience) [Goyal et al, 2018]
  • Attempts to influence in-group are costly [Ellwardt et al. 2016]

Framework: Identity change



  • • Individuals have group identities
    • They can change them
    • Sticky attributes signal previous membership

  • • Individuals relate with others through networks
    • Both in-group & out-group

  • • Want to coordinate with others: benefit
    • Prefer different outcomes: conflict
    • Identities shape preferences

  • • Social structures: Integration vs. segregation in the network
    • Efficiency: Individual and network gains from coordination

The Model: 3 stages


  • • There is a set of players: $N=\{1,2\}$
  • • Each player $i$ is assigned a group identity: $θ_i=\{●, ▲\}$, and
  • • An external attribute (appearance): $φ_i=\{◯, △\}$
  • • Every $ i ∈ N $ knows the group assigned to all other players: $θ=\{θ_1,θ_2,..., θ_n\}$
  • • The set of players by group: $N_{●}=\{\i ∈ N: θ_i = ●\}$ and $N_{▲}=\{\i ∈ N: θ_i = ▲\}$, with cardinality $|N_{●}|,|N_{▲}|$
  • • If $|N_{●}|≠|N_{▲}|$, the largest set is the majority

1. Group identity choice


  • • Endogenous choice of group: $\ov{θ}_i=\{●, ▲\}$
  • • $i$ can stay in the assigned group, $(\ov{θ}_i=θ_i)$, or change it, $(\ov{θ}_i ≠ θ_i)$
  • • The resulting vector of chosen groups is: $\ov{θ}=\{\ov{θ}_1,\ov{θ}_2,..., \ov{θ}_n\}$
  • • Finite set of all vectors: $\ov{Θ}$

2. Network formation


  • • Each player observes $\ov{θ}$
  • • Proposes connections to others: $g_i=(g_{i1},...,g_{ii-1},g_{ii+1},...,g_{in})$ where $g_{ij} ∈ \{0,1\}$
  • • $N_i\{g\}=\{\j ∈ N: g_{ij} = 1\}$ is $i$'s targeted neighbors (outdegree)
  • • Results in the directed network $g=(g_1,g_2,...,g_n)$
  • • If $g_{ij}=g_{ji}=1$ an undirected link $\ov{g}_{ij}$ is formed
  • • The resulting network $\ov{g}$ represents all bilateral connections: $N_i\{\ov{g}\}=\{\j ∈ N: \ov{g}_{ij} = 1\}$
  • • Finite set of all undirected networks: $\ov{G}$

3. Action chosen


  • • All players observe the resulting network (all proposals)
  • • $i$ chooses an action from a binary set $A=\{$p,b$\}$
  • • Group based preference: $● (▲)$ prefers p over b (b over p)
  • • $x_i(\ov{g})=\ov{θ}_i$ when $i$ chooses her preferred action, and $x_i(\ov{g}) ≠ \ov{θ}_i$ otherwise
  • • $i$'s strategy is defined through a total function $x_i:\ov{Θ},\ov{G} → A$

Utility (payoffs)


$$u_i(\ov{θ}_i,g,\ov{g},x)=λ^{\ov{θ}_i}_{x_i}(1+∑_{j ∈ N_i(\ov{g})} I_{\{x_j=x_i\}})- (c(\ov{θ}_i)+ k|N_i(g)|)$$

  • • where $∑_{j ∈ N_i(\ov{g})} I_{\{x_j=x_i\}}$ counts all neighbors choosing the same as $i$
  • • $λ^{\ov{θ}_i}_{x_i}=α$ if $x_i(\ov{g})=\ov{θ}_i$ (chooses her preferred action) and $λ^{\ov{θ}_i}_{x_i}=β$ otherwise: $α>β$
  • • switching cost: $c(\ov{θ}_i) ≥ 0$ if $i$ changed groups $(\ov{θ}_i ≠ θ_i)$, and $0$ otherwise
  • • Cost of proposing a link: $k>0$

Equilibrium charaterization (B.I.)

1. Actions: Fix the network. Players choose their preferred action if the proportion selecting the same is sufficiently large (threshold).
2. Connections: Every two players choosing the same action are connected, every two players connected are choosing the same action (Pairwise stability).

Equilibrium charaterization (B.I.)

1. Actions: Fix the network. Players choose their preferred action if the proportion selecting the same is sufficiently large (threshold).
2. Connections: Every two players choosing the same action are connected, every two players connected are choosing the same action (Pairwise stability).
3. Group choice: Each player's action is aligned with the chosen group identity.

Fixed vs. Fluid Group Identities

Effects on equilibria and efficiency

  • Equilibria

    Identity change shrinks the equilibrium set to outcomes where all players choose their preferred action
    *even compared to fixed identities with homogeneous populations.

  • Efficiency gains

    The efficient outcome (complete network with coordination on a single action) leads to larger gains with fluid identities.

    2 receivers: one per group

Conclusions & Discussion

Conclusions

  • Conflicting group identities are more likely to result in segregated inefficient outcomes
  • Being assigned to a disadvantaged group can impede escaping the inefficient path
  • If group identity is endogenous, individuals will strategically change it
    1. What if the switch is costly?
    2. What if the switch is unobservable but all know it is possible?
